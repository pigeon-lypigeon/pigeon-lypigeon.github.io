With hacking, we've all seen it in films: some sort of baggy hoodie or apparel, reflective glasses, lines of green code being written at ungodly speeds, techno thumping in the background, and depending on how dramatic the story is, maybe the occasional cut to some corporation heads freaking out.
The truth of the matter is that, (for the most part) this isn't how it is. Firstly, to what I know at least, most hackers are more sensible than going after expansive corporations with herculean legal teams, and secondly, not all hacking occurs with some evil plot or malicious plan behind the wheel. Hacking, as it turns out, is an important aspect of cybersecurity.

After all, if a certified person can enter a network, perhaps via a backdoor, or some exploit, then they can notify the team, and the software can be patched, hopefully preventing other people from entering that way. This hacking with the intent of improving security rather than exploiting it is known as "White Hat, (or Ethical) Hacking", and you can actually get certified for it!
